Ch 8

Planning Work Activities

Key Definitions
PlanningDefining goals, establishing strategies, and developing plans to integrate and coordinate activities.
Stated GoalsOfficial statements of what an organization says its goals are to stakeholders.
Real GoalsThe goals an organization actually pursues, as evidenced by what members do.
MBOManagement by Objectives — mutually agreed-upon goals used to evaluate performance.
Environmental ScanningScreening information to detect emerging trends in the external environment.
Why Planning Matters
Provides direction — aligns departments toward the same goals.
Reduces uncertainty — anticipates change before it happens.
Minimizes waste & redundancy — stops departments working at cross-purposes.
Sets control standards — the plan becomes the benchmark for evaluation.
Strategic vs. Operational Plans
FeatureStrategic PlansOperational Plans
BreadthEntire organizationParticular operational area
Time FrameLong-termShort-term
Goal FocusOverall goalsNarrow, specific goals
Specific vs. Directional Plans
FeatureSpecific PlansDirectional Plans
DefinitionClearly defined, no room for interpretationFlexible, set out general guidelines
When to UseStable environmentsHigh environmental uncertainty
Single-Use vs. Standing Plans
ConceptSingle-Use PlansStanding Plans
FrequencyUsed once for a unique situationUsed repeatedly for recurring activities
ExamplesBudgets, specific projectsPolicies, rules, procedures
🧠 Memory Trick
MBO goals are SPEC: Specificity · Participative · Explicit time period · Constant feedback
⭐ Exam Tips — High Yield
Differences between Stated vs. Real goals and Strategic vs. Operational plans are frequently tested.
⚠ Common Mistake
Students forget that planning must be flexible in dynamic environments — don't treat plans as fixed.
Ch 9

Competitive Advantage & Strategy

Key Definitions
Competitive AdvantageWhat sets an organization apart; its distinctive edge.
Competitive NecessityFactors required just to stay in the game, rather than to lead.
SWOT AnalysisEvaluation of Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats — the foundational strategy tool.
Levels of Strategy
Corporate — the overall direction of the entire business.
Competitive — how each business unit will compete in its specific market.
Functional — how specific departments (HR, Marketing) support the competitive strategy.
Porter's Competitive Strategies
Cost Leadership
Aim to be the lowest-cost producer in the industry.
Ex: Hotel minimizing costs for lowest room rates.
Differentiation
Offer unique products customers value and are willing to pay more for.
Ex: Hotel offering luxury/themed rooms.
Focus Strategy
Target a specific narrow segment or niche market rather than the broad industry.
⭐ Professor Emphasis
Know the link between competitive strategy → functional strategy (e.g., how HR policy changes to support a cost-leadership goal).
Ch 11

Designing Organizational Structure

Key Definitions
OrganizingArranging and structuring work to accomplish organizational goals.
Organizational StructureThe formal arrangement of jobs within an organization.
Chain of CommandThe line of authority extending from upper levels to the lowest levels.
Span of ControlThe number of subordinates a manager can efficiently and effectively manage.
The 6 Elements of Organizational Design
1. Work Specialization
Dividing work into separate job tasks.
2. Departmentalization
Grouping jobs (Functional, Product, Customer…)
3. Chain of Command
Line of authority top → bottom.
4. Span of Control
Number of direct reports per manager.
5. Centralization
Decision-making concentrated at the top vs. delegated down.
6. Formalization
Degree to which jobs are standardized and rule-governed.
Mechanistic vs. Organic
StructureCharacteristicsBest Used When…
MechanisticRigid, stable, tight controls, high formalizationEnvironment is stable; goal is cost control
OrganicFlexible, adaptive, free-flowing info, low formalizationEnvironment is uncertain; goal is innovation
🔑 Key Rule
Structure must follow strategy. You choose the design based on what the organization is trying to achieve.
Ch 12

Managing Human Resources

Key Definitions
HR PlanningEnsuring the right number and kinds of people are in the right places at the right times.
Affirmative ActionPrograms that enhance the status of protected groups.
SelectionScreening applicants to hire the most appropriate candidates.
The HRM Process
Planning — assess future HR needs.
Recruitment — locate and attract capable applicants.
Decruitment — reduce the workforce (layoffs, attrition, early retirement).
Selection — screen and hire the best fit.
Orientation — onboard and introduce to the organization.
Training — develop skills for current and future roles.
Performance Management — evaluate, give feedback, and reward.
Retention — keep high performers engaged.
Recruitment vs. Decruitment
ConceptRecruitmentDecruitment
GoalLocate and attract capable applicantsReduce the workforce
MethodsReferrals, ads, social mediaLayoffs, attrition, early retirement
⭐ Why HR = Competitive Advantage
HR cannot be easily imitated — skilled, motivated people are a resource rivals can't simply copy overnight.
Ch 17–18

Leading & Controlling

Key Definitions
LeaderSomeone who can influence others and has managerial authority.
ControllingMonitoring, comparing, and correcting work performance against set standards.
The 3-Step Control Process
Measure actual performance (quantitative or qualitative).
Compare performance against the predetermined standard.
Take action — correct significant deviations.
Types of Corrective Action
Immediate Corrective Action — fixes the problem at once without investigating root cause.
Basic Corrective Action — investigates why and how the deviation occurred, then corrects the source.
📌 Key Concept
The Range of Variation determines when a deviation is significant enough to require action — not every gap from standard needs a response.
Module

Managing Change & Disruptive Innovation

Key Definitions
Change AgentA catalyst who initiates and manages the change process.
VUCAVolatility, Uncertainty, Complexity, Ambiguity — the modern operating environment.
CreativityCombining ideas in a unique way. (The idea.)
InnovationTurning creative ideas into useful products or methods. (The result.)
Two Metaphors for Change
🌊 Calm Waters (Lewin)
Unfreeze → prepare people for change
Change → implement the new approach
Refreeze → sustain and stabilize
🌪 White-Water Rapids
Constant disruption is the norm. Managers must learn and adapt continuously — there is no stable state to refreeze into.
Techniques to Reduce Resistance
TechniqueBest When…Example
Education + CommunicationMisinformation existsExplaining ERP benefits in multiple languages
ParticipationPeople have relevant expertiseLetting staff redesign customer flow
CoercionUrgent legal or safety issuesMandating new safety protocols
⚠ Common Mistake
Don't confuse Creativity (the idea) with Innovation (making it real and useful). They are not the same thing.
⭐ High Yield
Sustaining vs. Disruptive Innovation — Sustaining improves existing products for existing customers. Disruptive radically changes industry rules, often serving a different or new market first.
High-Yield Summary
Change is the "operating system," not a one-time event.
Managers change Strategy, Structure, Technology, and People.
Resistance stems from uncertainty, habit, fear of loss, and goal mismatch.
Reducing resistance requires communication, participation, and support.
Disruptive innovation radically changes industry rules from the bottom up.
